# Summary: Deciphering Secrets of Ancient Civilizations, Noah's Ark, and Flood Myths

**Guest:** Irving Finkel (British Museum Curator)  
**Host:** Lex Fridman  
**Duration:** ~3.5 hours  
**[Watch Full Video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ)**

## Overview

Irving Finkel, curator at the British Museum for 45+ years and world expert on cuneiform, discusses the origins of writing in Mesopotamia, decipherment of ancient languages, controversial archaeological theories, the Epic of Gilgamesh, the Flood Myth and Noah's Ark tablet, ancient board games, the nature of religion and humanity, and the sacred mission of the British Museum to preserve human cultural heritage.

---

## Topics with Timestamps

### [00:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=0s) - Introduction to Irving Finkel
Irving Finkel is introduced as a British Museum curator specializing in cuneiform—the wedge-shaped ancient writing system invented in Mesopotamia around 3500 BC that lasted over 3,000 years.

### [01:30](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=90s) - Origins of Writing in Mesopotamia
Discussion of how writing emerged from accounting needs—tracking sheep, grain, and commodities. The earliest tablets are bureaucratic records, not literature. Writing developed from pictographs into abstract signs representing syllables.

### [06:15](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=375s) - Göbekli Tepe Controversy
Finkel presents his controversial theory that pictographic writing existed as early as 9000 BC at Göbekli Tepe in Turkey, potentially 6,000 years before traditional Sumerian origins. He uses the "raindrop inferring Niagara Falls" principle—if sophisticated writing existed in 3500 BC, simpler forms must have preceded it.

### [12:45](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=765s) - Development of Lexicography
Ancient scribes invented lexicography—systematically organizing signs by categories (wood items, reed items, colors, countries, gods). This standardization enabled cuneiform's 3,000+ year longevity across multiple languages and empires.

### [19:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=1160s) - 19th Century Discoveries
How cuneiform tablets were discovered in 19th century Iraq—scattered on ancient library floors, preserved in dry climate. Ashurbanipal's library at Nineveh contained thousands of tablets covering literature, science, omen texts, and more.

### [26:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=1600s) - Decipherment Breakthrough: The Behistun Inscription
The trilingual Behistun inscription (Old Persian, Elamite, Babylonian) served as the Rosetta Stone for cuneiform. Darius the Great's proclamation enabled scholars like Henry Rawlinson and Edward Hincks to crack the code in the 1850s.

### [33:15](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=1995s) - Structure of Cuneiform Writing
Explanation of cuneiform as a syllabic system with consonant+vowel combinations (ba, ab, bi, ib) and multivalent signs. Unlike alphabets, one sign can represent multiple sounds depending on context.

### [40:30](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=2430s) - Learning Cuneiform at University
Finkel's experience learning cuneiform at University of Birmingham under Professor Lambert. The challenges of translation including modal verbs (could/might/should) that aren't expressed grammatically in Akkadian.

### [46:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=2780s) - Chicago Assyrian Dictionary
Praise for the Chicago Assyrian Dictionary as "America's greatest cultural achievement" alongside the electric guitar. Discussion of translation philosophy—capturing subtlety and nuance beyond literal word-for-word translation.

### [52:10](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=3130s) - Ancient Literature Examples
Delightful examples: love letters comparing features to radishes and walruses, street dramas with jealous goddesses, everyday contracts and royal accounts. Literature reveals human nature unchanged across millennia.

### [57:45](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=3465s) - Mesopotamian Pantheon and Religious Beliefs
Structure of Mesopotamian religion: top tier gods (Anu, Enlil, Ea) and hundreds of lesser deities. Personal patron gods required sacrifices and bribes. Gods were taken for granted like natural phenomena—not believed in but accepted as reality.

### [01:04:15](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=3855s) - Critique of Monotheism
Finkel's controversial view: monotheistic religions brought evil into the world through dogmatic "I'm right, you're wrong" thinking, leading to Inquisitions and religious violence. Polytheistic systems were more tolerant.

### [01:05:36](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=3936s) - Epic of Gilgamesh
Discussion of literature's earliest epic: Gilgamesh was a real king of Uruk whose stories evolved from oral tradition (1800s BC) into sophisticated 12-tablet Assyrian version. Themes of humanity, immortality, and humans in the hands of gods. Contains oral literature markers like "Gilgamesh opened his mouth to speak..."

### [01:11:22](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=4282s) - The Ark Tablet Discovery
Finkel's famous decoding of the Babylonian flood story tablet (1700 BC)—predating the Biblical Noah's Ark by at least 1,000 years. A visitor brought the tablet to the British Museum; Finkel immediately recognized it as flood narrative instructions.

### [01:13:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=4380s) - Details of the Flood Myth
Gods decided to wipe out noisy humanity. Ea warned Atrahasis to build a boat—describing materials, measurements, and the revolutionary discovery: the ark was ROUND (a giant coracle), not the traditional coffin shape from Bible illustrations.

### [01:15:01](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=4501s) - Literary Dependence on Mesopotamia
George Smith's 1872 discovery that the Gilgamesh tablets contained the same flood story as Genesis—even the detail of releasing three birds. This proved literary dependence: the Biblical flood story was borrowed from Mesopotamian sources.

### [01:16:25](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=4585s) - Mesopotamian Origin of Flood Myths
Geography matters: Jerusalem doesn't get floods, but Mesopotamian rivers flooded catastrophically. Finkel theorizes one ancient tsunami created collective memory passed down through generations, eventually codified in writing.

### [01:17:23](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=4643s) - Flood Myths Across Cultures
Why flood myths appear globally: Mesopotamian story spread through literary transmission (missionaries, merchants) and independent origin (rivers flood everywhere). Rejects Graham Hancock's Younger Dryas hypothesis of global cataclysm 10,000 BC.

### [01:20:46](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=4846s) - Biblical Composition Theory
Theory that Hebrew Bible was written during Babylonian exile (6th century BC) by Judean refugees. They borrowed Mesopotamian creation/flood stories but adapted them—changing "noisy people" to "sinful people" to fit monotheistic theology.

### [01:21:29](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=4889s) - Building the Ark Replica
Finkel commissioned British boat builders in Kerala, India to construct a 1/3 scale replica using the tablet's specifications. Wooden ribs, woven reeds, waterproofed with bitumen. The boat floated successfully (with authentic leaking!). Documentary captured the process.

### [01:24:42](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=5082s) - "Before the Flood" as Historical Marker
Mesopotamians used "before the flood" vs "after the flood" as temporal marker—like "before the war" in British culture. King lists included pre-flood rulers with impossibly long reigns. The flood served as civilization's reset point in collective memory.

### [01:31:42](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=5502s) - Flood Story: From Oral to Written
The flood narrative contains precise measurements of boat-building materials—suggesting oral performers visited villages with coracle expertise, calculated real proportions, then scaled them up dramatically to satisfy knowledgeable audiences. This reveals the transition from oral to written literature.

### [01:32:50](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=5570s) - The Royal Game of Ur
Discovery of 2,600 BC board game from royal tombs at Ur (found by Leonard Woolley 1920s). The game spread across the Middle East for 3,000 years—found in Egypt (Tutankhamun's tomb), Turkey, Greece, Crete, Syria, Lebanon. A 20-square race game combining chance (dice) and strategy.

### [01:39:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=5960s) - Decoding Ancient Game Rules
Finkel discovered a 2nd century BC tablet in the British Museum with game rules—names of pieces, throws, movements. He reconstructed playable rules that people worldwide now use. The game balances probability and strategy like backgammon.

### [01:41:44](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=6104s) - Board Games and Human Nature
Games sublimate rivalry and competition into safe terrain. Time pass (Indian concept)—what you do when it's too hot to work. Ancient peoples had same creative impulses, rivalries, and need for stimulation. Board games are embedded in culture from very early times.

### [01:45:52](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=6352s) - The British Museum's Mission
Finkel's passionate defense: The British Museum is unique globally—celebrating ALL human achievement from the beginning, not one nation's art. It serves the entire world impartially and the unborn future. Free admission, scientific preservation, narrative of humanity's struggle and triumph.

### [01:49:06](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=6546s) - Museums as Lighthouses
Museums are "lighthouses in a universe of darkness, ignorance, stupidity." The British Museum doesn't favor religions or nations—it examines them dispassionately. Future generations will reinterpret objects differently; distance enables sharper understanding.

### [01:53:15](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=6795s) - Ancient vs Modern Wisdom
Ancient peoples were fundamentally the same—"put them on a bus wearing same clothes, you wouldn't know." But they lived more naturally without electronic distractions. They knew astronomy, told stories by firelight, created cave paintings of unsurpassable quality demonstrating genius existed from the beginning.

### [01:55:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=6900s) - Electronic Universe as Addiction
Finkel's critique: Modern adherence to electronics is "disastrous for humans"—reducing vitality of human component. Like drugs, people don't realize addiction until too late. Ancient life allowed humans to behave more naturally.

### [01:57:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=7020s) - Language and Civilization
Passionate defense of proper English vocabulary: Children worldwide cannot articulate using "the most literary and beneficial language ever created." Example: "like I went" as meaningless filler. Reading literature is how you build vocabulary; conversation alone won't do it.

### [01:59:32](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=7172s) - The Oxford English Dictionary
Most people who "speak English" haven't opened the full Oxford English Dictionary—"you might turn several pages before finding a single word you've heard before." English is unimaginably rich. Proper vocabulary is crucial because words define the quality of your thoughts.

### [02:01:34](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=7294s) - 130,000 Cuneiform Tablets
The British Museum houses approximately 130,000 cuneiform tablets. Few jokes survived in writing (example: mosquito lands on elephant asking "am I too heavy for you?"). Finkel references Tom Lehrer's wit and mathematical genius.

### [02:04:28](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bBRVNkAfkQ&t=7468s) - Closing: Invitation to the Museum
Finkel invites Lex to visit the British Museum for personal tour of cuneiform collection, the Chicago Assyrian Dictionary, and grammar books. Final tribute to meeting a remarkable human being who has dedicated his life to preserving and understanding humanity's earliest written heritage.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Writing originated from bureaucracy** (~3500 BC Mesopotamia), not literature or religion—tracking sheep and grain led to one of humanity's greatest inventions.

2. **Controversial early writing theory**: Finkel proposes pictographic writing existed as early as 9000 BC at Göbekli Tepe, potentially rewriting the timeline of human communication by 6,000 years.

3. **Cuneiform lasted 3,000+ years** across multiple civilizations because ancient scribes invented lexicography—systematic organization enabling standardization and teaching.

4. **The Flood Story proves literary dependence**: The 1700 BC Ark Tablet predates the Biblical Noah by at least 1,000 years, with identical details (three birds, boat specifications) proving the Hebrew Bible borrowed from Mesopotamian sources.

5. **The Ark was ROUND**: Revolutionary discovery—Noah's vessel was a giant coracle (circular boat), not the traditional ship shape, based on real Mesopotamian watercraft technology.

6. **Ancient peoples were fundamentally identical to us**: Same intelligence, creativity, emotions, moral struggles—they painted unsurpassable cave art, played sophisticated board games, wrote love letters and jokes, and navigated the same human condition.

7. **The Royal Game of Ur spread for 3,000 years** across the ancient world without written rules or common language—demonstrating universal human desire for games balancing chance and strategy.

8. **Monotheism brought dogmatism and evil**: Finkel controversially argues polytheistic religions were more tolerant; monotheistic "I'm right, you're wrong" thinking led to Inquisitions and religious violence throughout history.

9. **The British Museum serves the unborn**: Its unique global mission is celebrating ALL human achievement impartially, preserving artifacts for future generations who will reinterpret them with fresh perspectives.

10. **Language is sacred, vocabulary defines thought**: Proper command of rich vocabulary is crucial for clear thinking and communication—reading literature is essential because conversation alone won't build linguistic sophistication.
